What Schools Actually Need (And Why We Built Our Programs Around That)

We talked to 40+ school principals before building our in-school programs. This is what they told us — and how it changed what we built.

M
Maekido Team · 11 min read
◆

One of the principals who shaped how we think about what we build

Before we built a single curriculum module, we made a decision: we wouldn't design programs based on what we thought schools needed. We'd ask.

Over four months, we spoke to 43 school principals across six cities — from private schools in South Mumbai to government schools in the outskirts of Hyderabad. We asked them the same questions. We listened for the patterns. What we heard changed almost everything we'd planned to build.

What We Expected to Hear

We went in expecting principals to tell us they needed "21st century skills" training, coding programs, and robotics workshops. That's the dominant narrative in education technology right now, and we assumed it would reflect what schools were actually asking for.

We were mostly wrong.

The most common thing principals said — in different words, across very different schools — was this: "Our students don't believe they're capable of anything beyond what the exam asks for."

What They Actually Said

Here are four of the voices that shaped how we built Maekido's programs. Names have been changed, but the words are real.

🏫

Principal, government school, Hyderabad

700 students · Classes 6–10
"We have smart children. Very smart. But they've been trained to think that their job is to remember what I tell them. When I ask them to have an opinion, they look at me like I've asked them to do something dangerous. That's what I need help changing."
🏢

Principal, private school, Pune

1,200 students · Classes 1–12
"Parents ask me for coding programs, AI programs, robotics. I put those in. And the students learn the tools. But they don't know what to build with them. No one has taught them to ask a question that the tool should answer. That's the actual gap."
🏫

Principal, girls' school, Chennai

450 students · Classes 6–12
"My girls perform exceptionally on exams. They go quiet in group discussions. They don't argue, they don't question. They're brilliant at answering — and terrified of being wrong. I need something that teaches them that being wrong is the beginning of something, not the end."
🏗️

Principal, CBSE school, Bengaluru

850 students · Classes 1–10
"Every vendor who comes to me talks about what their program teaches. Nobody asks me what my students are going home and doing differently after the program. That's the only thing I actually care about."

The Pattern We Heard

Across 43 conversations, the themes were strikingly consistent:

What principals said they actually needed:

78% Said their students struggled with open-ended problems where there was no single right answer
71% Said students were afraid of public failure — in discussions, presentations, peer feedback
83% Said they wanted programs that changed how students saw themselves, not just what they could do
69% Said previous external programs left no visible change in student behavior after they ended

How This Changed What We Built

The conversations pushed us to build around a different question. Not "what skills do students need?" but "what do students need to believe about themselves before any skill will stick?"

Four beliefs emerged as foundational:

🎯

I am capable of figuring things out

Not just following instructions — actually working through an ambiguous problem without a guaranteed right answer. This belief is the prerequisite for everything else.

🔁

Being wrong is information, not verdict

Students who see mistakes as evidence of their inadequacy stop taking risks. Students who see mistakes as data points become iterators. The difference is enormous.

◆

A student in Pune working through their third iteration of a board game design

🗣️

My perspective has value

Students who have been trained to find the teacher's answer often stop forming their own. Rebuilding the belief that your own perspective is worth contributing is one of the deepest things a program can do.

🔗

What I learn here applies beyond here

The Bengaluru principal was right: a program that only works inside its own session has failed. Every Maekido session is designed so the thing practiced can be picked up and used outside the room.

What This Looks Like In Practice

These four beliefs show up in every design decision we make — including ones that look small from the outside:

We don't use points or rankings in our programs, because they shift the goal from making something real to outscoring someone else. We don't give model answers or "the best" examples at the start of a session, because showing students the ceiling before they've tried to build their own tends to make them copy rather than create. We always end sessions with a "what would you do differently?" question — not because we're evaluating students, but because iteration is a habit we're building.

What We're Still Getting Wrong

This transparency goes both ways. Even with 43 principal conversations as our foundation, we're still making mistakes.

We struggle with programs that run only once. A single session can spark something, but it's rarely enough to change a belief. We know this. We're still working on how to build lasting presence in schools that don't have budget for multi-session programs.

We also haven't solved the evaluation problem. Our programs change how students feel about creative work — we can measure that. We're less good at showing the downstream impact on academic performance, social confidence, or long-term creative practice. Those things matter, and we owe the schools that work with us better evidence.

The Principles We're Holding

We came out of those 43 conversations with principles we've tried to hold even when they've made things harder:

Design from the actual need, not the perceived need. The market says schools need coding. The principals say schools need students who believe they're capable of thinking. We build for what the principals said.

Behavior change over content delivery. We care less about what students can list after a program and more about what they're doing differently. A student who signs up for an art elective is more valuable data than a student who scores well on a design quiz.

No program is better than a bad program. A program that runs once, leaves nothing behind, and lets a school tick a box for "creative education" does active harm. It uses up the goodwill, the budget, and the student enthusiasm that a better program could have used. We'd rather not run in a school than run something extractive.

The schools told us what they needed. Our only job is to keep building toward it — and to stay honest about how far we still have to go.
