Why Board Game Design is the Best Systems Thinking Class You've Never Tried

When students have to design the rules, the balance, and the fun of a game — they're doing systems thinking without knowing it. Here's why it works.

✍️
Maekido Team · 7 min read
🎲

Students playtesting their own board games mid-design

Ask a student what "systems thinking" means and you'll get a blank stare. Ask them to design a board game that's actually fun to play, and they'll spend three hours debating whether drawing two cards is too powerful, whether the penalty for landing on a red square feels fair, and whether the win condition makes the endgame exciting or boring.

That's systems thinking. They just don't know that's what it's called.

What Systems Thinking Actually Means (And Why Schools Struggle to Teach It)

Systems thinking is the ability to see how individual parts interact to create a whole — and to predict what happens when one part changes. It's a crucial skill for everything from climate science to business strategy to engineering. And it's notoriously difficult to teach in a classroom setting.

The standard approach involves diagrams, flowcharts, and long explanations about feedback loops. Students understand the vocabulary. They struggle to feel what it actually means for a system to be balanced — or broken.

A game is a system you can hold in your hands and break on purpose. That's the key insight that makes board game design such a powerful teaching tool.

Traditional Systems Thinking Class

  • ✗ Abstract diagrams
  • ✗ Hypothetical feedback loops
  • ✗ Hard to feel consequences
  • ✗ Passive learning
  • ✓ Good vocabulary

Board Game Design Class

  • ✓ Live, playable systems
  • ✓ Immediate feedback on decisions
  • ✓ Feel the imbalance instantly
  • ✓ Actively iterating & testing
  • ✓ Builds intuition, not just knowledge

The Design Process Is the Curriculum

Here's what a typical board game design session looks like in a Maekido classroom:

1

Define the Core Experience

What should playing this game feel like? Tense? Funny? Collaborative? Students have to articulate an intended emotional experience before designing a single rule.

2

Draft the Ruleset

Write the minimum rules needed to play. Students quickly discover that ambiguous rules lead to arguments — and that precise language is a design tool.

3

Playtest & Break It

Play the game with the explicit goal of finding what's broken or boring. This is where systems thinking happens naturally — "if I do this, then that happens, and then the game is over in two minutes."

4

Iterate & Rebalance

Change one variable and test again. Students learn intuitively that changing one part of a system changes everything else — the heart of systems thinking.

5

Teach It to Someone Else

If another student can't learn the game in five minutes, the rules aren't clear enough. Teaching is the final test of whether the system makes sense.

What Happened in Our Classroom

In our pilot session, a group of four students decided to design a resource management game set in a fictional city. They gave each player a role: Mayor, Engineer, Teacher, and Doctor. Each role had different goals and different resources.

Within twenty minutes of their first playtest, the Engineer was winning every single time — by a huge margin. The students had accidentally made one role so powerful that the game wasn't fun.

⚖️

The heated "balancing session" — debating resource distribution rules

What followed was one of the most engaged academic conversations we'd seen. Four students sat in a circle, arguing about whether the problem was the Engineer's starting resources, the victory conditions, the action economy, or some combination of all three. They ran six iterations in forty minutes.

They had no idea they were learning systems thinking. They thought they were fixing a game.

The Skills That Transfer

After a four-week board game design unit, here's what students consistently demonstrate that they've internalized:

Cause and effect in complex systems. Change one rule, everything shifts. Students stop thinking linearly and start thinking in webs of consequence.

The difference between complicated and complex. A game with 100 rules isn't necessarily fun or smart. A simple, elegant ruleset that produces surprising depth — that's design mastery.

Iteration as a practice, not a failure. The first version of anything is a hypothesis. Testing it doesn't mean you failed; it means you're learning.

🏆

Final showcase day — students presenting their finished games

Why This Works for All Kinds of Learners

One of the most important things we've noticed: board game design reaches students who don't typically shine in traditional classrooms. The student who struggles with essays turns out to have a gift for elegant rule design. The student who hates math is suddenly obsessed with probability and resource balancing.

Games are inherently democratic. You can be a great game designer whether you're a visual thinker, a verbal thinker, a social learner, or a lone tinkerer. There's a role for every kind of intelligence.

"I never thought I was smart until I built something that other people wanted to play." — student reflection, age 13

Want to Try It?

You don't need a budget, a special curriculum, or prior design experience to run a board game design session. You need cardstock, pencils, and a willingness to let students discover what doesn't work. The learning happens in the mess.
