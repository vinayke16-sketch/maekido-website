5 Things We Learned Running Creative Programs for the First Time

Summer 2026 was our first major season. Here are five things we didn't expect — and how they've shaped every decision we've made since.

✍️
Founder, Maekido · 10 min read
💡

Our team after the final session of Summer 2026 — exhausted and certain

A note before we start: This is an honest piece. We didn't get everything right in our first season. Some of what we learned was uncomfortable. We're sharing it anyway because we think the mistakes are as instructive as the wins — for us and for anyone else building something similar.

When we launched Maekido's first full season of in-school creative programs, we had a curriculum we believed in, facilitators we trusted, and a level of optimism that, in retrospect, was both necessary and slightly delusional.

Summer 2026 ran across 11 schools. We ran programs in Mandala art, board game design, UI/UX, and legal literacy. We worked with nearly 400 students. Here's what five months of that actually taught us.

Lesson 01

The Teacher's Buy-In Matters More Than the Student's

We went in thinking the hardest thing would be getting students engaged. We were wrong. The hardest thing was getting the classroom teacher on board — and the programs where the teacher was genuinely excited looked completely different from the ones where they were just present.

In the schools where teachers leaned in — sat down with students, tried the activity themselves, contributed to discussions — the energy in the room was electric. Students were more willing to take risks. The environment felt permissive of being wrong.

In the schools where teachers sat at the back checking their phones, the students were fine. But "fine" is a very different thing from "transformed." The teacher sets the emotional weather of a room, even when they're not leading the session.

What we changed: We now run a 30-minute teacher briefing before every program — not to explain the curriculum, but to invite teachers to participate as learners themselves. The ones who show up to that briefing curious are the ones who make the sessions exceptional.
👩‍🏫

A teacher joining a board game design session as a player — the moment things shifted

Lesson 02

Students Don't Need More Content — They Need More Time

We packed our original curriculum with material. Every session had learning objectives, activities, reflection exercises, and a closing discussion. It looked great on paper. In practice, we kept running out of time before we got to the things that actually mattered.

The best moments in every program — the ones students talked about afterwards, the ones that showed up in their reflections — weren't the planned activities. They were the conversations that emerged when we let something run longer than scheduled. The student who asked a hard question. The debate that broke out during a board game playtest. The girl who started crying during a legal literacy case study because the scenario was too close to something her family had experienced.

Those moments can't be scheduled. They need slack in the schedule to breathe.

What we changed: We cut our content by 30% and built in what we now call "open space" — unstructured time in every session where the facilitator can follow what's emerging rather than what's planned.
Lesson 03

The Students Who Struggled Most Were the High-Achievers

This one surprised us more than anything else. We expected students who were behind academically to find our programs challenging. Instead, the students who most consistently struggled with creative work were the top-of-class students.

The pattern was consistent: high-achieving students were deeply uncomfortable with the absence of a right answer. In a Mandala session, they'd ask whether their petal size was "correct." In the UI/UX workshop, they'd want to know which design was "better." In legal literacy case studies, they'd want a definitive ruling before the discussion had even started.

They weren't lazy or disengaged. They were anxious. They'd spent years in environments that rewarded the right answer, and we were asking them to sit with ambiguity. That's a different kind of challenge — and a more important one for their development.

🧩

A high-achiever discovering that "correct" isn't always a useful question in design

What we changed: We now explicitly name the discomfort at the start of programs — "There are no right answers here, and that might feel strange. That strangeness is part of what we're here to practice." Naming it doesn't eliminate it, but it makes it easier to sit with.
Lesson 04

You Learn What Actually Worked About Two Weeks After the Program Ends

We did exit surveys. Students said lovely things. We felt good. Then, two to four weeks later, we started hearing the real stories — from teachers, from parents, occasionally from students themselves. And those stories were different from the exit survey responses.

A student who'd said she "learned a lot about design" had actually gone home and redesigned her bedroom. A student who'd said the legal literacy program was "interesting" had used his knowledge to help his family navigate an issue with a landlord. A student who'd been quiet throughout the Mandala workshop had started teaching the technique to her younger siblings.

Real learning doesn't show up immediately. It shows up when it gets applied — and that's almost never in the moment of the session itself.

What we changed: We now do a follow-up touchpoint with teachers 30 days after each program. Not to measure what students remember, but to ask what students have done. Action is a better measure of learning than recall.
Lesson 05

The Schools That Didn't "Need" It Benefited the Most — But the Schools That Did Were Harder to Reach

Our programs were most enthusiastically received by schools that already had strong arts programs, engaged teachers, and supportive leadership. Which makes sense — those environments are already primed for this kind of work.

The schools where students had the least access to creative learning — under-resourced schools, overcrowded classrooms, teachers stretched beyond capacity — were the hardest to get into and the most logistically difficult to run programs in. And yet the impact, when we did get in, was incomparably larger.

🏫

A session in a government school in Hyderabad — one of our most meaningful programs of the season

A student who's already had three art workshops reacts differently to a fourth than a student who's never had one. For the first student, it's a good experience. For the second, it's a door opening onto something they didn't know existed.

What we changed: We've committed that at least 40% of our programs in every season will run in schools with limited or no existing creative programs. This makes our logistics harder. We think it's non-negotiable.

Year Two Starts Here

We're heading into our second full season with five hard-won lessons and a much healthier appreciation for how much we still don't know. If you're building something similar — in education, in creative programming, in community work — we hope something here is useful. And if you want to compare notes, our door is open.
