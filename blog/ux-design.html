What Happened When We Taught 9th Graders UI/UX Design

We weren't sure what to expect. Some students had never even heard of Figma. Here's what actually happened over two weeks — the good, the hard, and the surprising.

M
Maekido Team · 8 min read
◆

Students at work on their first wireframes

Last spring, we walked into a classroom of 28 ninth graders with a wild idea: what if students who had never opened a design tool could build something real — something they could actually use — in just two weeks?

The answer surprised us. Not because it went perfectly. It didn't. But because of what happened when it didn't go perfectly, and how students responded when the pressure was on.

Day One: The Blank Screen Problem

The first challenge wasn't teaching Figma. It was teaching students that there's no single "right answer" in design. Most of them came in expecting a formula — a step-by-step process that would produce something beautiful if followed correctly.

When we handed them a blank artboard and said "design an app that solves a problem you actually have," the room went very quiet.

"I kept waiting for her to tell us what to do. She just kept asking us what WE wanted. That was actually kind of scary." — 9th grader, age 14

◆

Students exploring the Figma interface for the first time

The blank screen is something designers at every level struggle with. But 14-year-olds, who are used to being handed a problem with a correct answer, found it particularly disorienting — and ultimately, liberating.

What They Actually Built

Over two weeks, students prototyped apps for everything from tracking school lunch menus to a "homework swap" platform where students could trade help on subjects they were strong in. One group designed a mental health check-in app for students who wanted a private way to signal when they were having a hard day.

That last one stopped us cold. We hadn't prompted it. They'd identified the need themselves.

The Moment Things Clicked

Midway through week one, a student named Riya came to us frustrated. She'd been trying to make her app "look professional" by copying design trends she'd seen on Instagram. Everything looked sleek — and felt empty.

We asked her one question: "Who are you designing this for?"

She stared at her screen for a while. Then she said: "Other girls like me who feel nervous in new places."

◆

The "who are you designing for?" conversation — the turning point

Within an hour, she'd scrapped the trendy UI and rebuilt it with warmer colors, larger text, and a friendlier tone. It wasn't perfect. But it was real. And she could explain every single decision she'd made, because she'd made them for someone.

What We Didn't Expect

We expected students to struggle with tools. They did, briefly. What we didn't expect was how quickly they started thinking like designers — questioning assumptions, testing with each other, arguing about whether a button placement "felt right."

We also didn't expect the emotional investment. Students who, by their own description, "hated art class" spent lunch breaks voluntarily iterating on their prototypes. One student told us he'd gone home and redesigned his entire app three times because "it still doesn't feel right."

"Design isn't about making things pretty. It's about making things make sense." — observation from a student's final reflection

The Hard Part

Let's be honest about what was difficult. Two weeks is not enough time to teach design thinking deeply. We were constantly making tradeoffs between going slow and going broad. Some students barely scratched the surface of what Figma can do. A few prototypes were more wireframe than design.

There was also the challenge of feedback. Students weren't used to being critiqued on subjective work. The first few critique sessions were tense — not because students were defensive, but because they genuinely didn't know that "this could be better" wasn't a personal failure.

Peer critique sessions — learning to give and receive design feedback

What We're Taking Forward

We're running this again. Next time, we're adding a third week focused exclusively on user testing — actually putting prototypes in front of real people and watching what happens. We're also building in more time for the "who are you designing for?" question at the very beginning, because that's where the best work started.

If you're an educator thinking about bringing design into your classroom, here's what we'd tell you: don't wait until you have the perfect curriculum. Give students a real problem, a real tool, and the permission to be wrong. The rest tends to take care of itself.

These students proved something important: the ability to think like a designer — to empathize, to question, to iterate — isn't a talent. It's a practice. And it can start at 14.
