The Student Who Said She Wasn't Artistic (Until She Made a Mandala)

She almost didn't sign up. "I can't draw," she said. Three hours into her first Mandala session, she hadn't looked up once. This is her story.

Priya's first mandala — completed in session three

Priya is 15. She is precise, methodical, and — by her own firm declaration — "not artistic." She took biology notes in color-coded columns. She made spreadsheets for her study schedule. She had never voluntarily made anything that could be called art.

When her school signed up for Maekido's Mandala Art workshop, Priya almost didn't go. Her friend convinced her to come for the first session. "Just to see," she said. "You can always leave."

She did not leave.

Why Priya Said She Couldn't Draw

The story Priya had about herself — "I'm not artistic" — is one we hear constantly. It usually traces back to a specific moment. For Priya, it was a class art project in fifth grade, when her painting of a landscape was held up as an example of "what not to do." She laughed about it when she told us. But she'd believed it for five years.

The problem isn't that children aren't artistic. The problem is that the version of art they're taught in school is narrow. It rewards a specific kind of talent — realistic representation, fine motor precision, a particular sense of proportion — and calls that "art." Everything else gets quietly filed under "not good at this."

Mandala art doesn't ask you to be good at drawing. It asks you to follow a pattern, one step at a time. The complexity emerges from repetition, not talent. That's why it works for people who've been told they can't create.

Session One: The Suspicion Phase

Priya's first session was mostly suspicion. She watched others, held her pen carefully, and checked her circles against the guide with an almost scientific precision. She didn't believe it would look good. She kept checking.

About ninety minutes in, she stopped checking. She was deep in the rhythm of repeating a petal pattern. When she looked up, the table was quiet. Everyone was absorbed. She looked at what she'd made.

"It didn't look like mine," she told us later. "It looked like something I'd see in a shop."

What Changed By Session Three

By the third session, Priya had started experimenting. She was no longer following the template exactly. She was varying the size of repeating elements, introducing a second color into a pattern that was supposed to use one, choosing a different line weight than the one demonstrated.

When we pointed this out, she seemed surprised. "I was just doing what felt right," she said.

That's what creative confidence sounds like when it starts to arrive. Not "I'm an artist now." Just: doing what feels right, and trusting it.

Careful, precise, checking constantly. Following every instruction exactly. Producing a beautiful first mandala and not believing it was hers.

Faster now. Less checking. Starting to trust the repetition. Trying a slightly different spacing on one pattern. Deciding she likes it.

Deviating from the template. Adding a second colour uninstructed. Making the outermost ring her own. Asking to take extra supplies home.

4

Session 4 — Full ownership

Designing from memory. Helping another student who was frustrated. Saying, entirely unprompted: "I actually think I'm good at this."

"I actually think I'm good at this."

— Priya, age 15, Session 4

Why This Matters Beyond the Mandala

The mandala itself isn't the point. The point is what it represents: a student who had a fixed story about herself — "I'm not creative" — and who now has evidence that the story was wrong.

That shift is harder to achieve than it sounds. Fixed self-narratives are powerful. They determine what we sign up for, what we try, what we give ourselves permission to enjoy. A student who believes she isn't artistic will avoid creative challenges for the rest of her life. A student who has proven herself wrong once has a very different relationship with risk.

Priya's Next Chapter

After the workshop ended, Priya asked if she could join the advanced Mandala session. She also, for the first time in her memory, chose art as her elective for the next term.

She's still precise and methodical. She still color-codes her notes. But she no longer says she can't draw. She says: "I haven't tried that yet."

That's a different kind of sentence. It leaves things open. It leaves room for being surprised.

"I used to think creativity was something you either had or you didn't. Now I think it's something that happens when you stop worrying about being bad at it." — Priya, post-program reflection
