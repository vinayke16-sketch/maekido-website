Project Abhay: Why Every School Should Teach Legal Literacy

Students know about gravity, photosynthesis, and the periodic table. But most don't know what rights they have under the Constitution. That needs to change.

✍️
Maekido Legal Team · 9 min read
⚖️

Students from a Delhi school participating in a mock constitutional hearing

A 16-year-old student in Mumbai was detained by local police for taking photographs near a government building. He had no idea that as a citizen, he had the legal right to photograph public spaces. He spent six hours at the station before his parents arrived — terrified, silent, certain he'd done something wrong.

He hadn't. He just didn't know his rights.

This is why we built Project Abhay. Abhay, from Sanskrit, means fearlessness. And our belief is simple: you can't be fearless about rights you don't know you have.

The Gap We're Filling

Indian students study the Constitution in class. They memorize Preamble excerpts for exams. But constitutional knowledge and constitutional literacy are very different things.

Knowledge is knowing that Article 19 guarantees freedom of speech. Literacy is knowing what that actually means when a school principal tells you not to publish an article in the student newsletter, or when a police officer tells you to stop filming in a public place.

A right you don't know how to exercise isn't really a right at all. It's just a sentence in a document.

In a survey we conducted across 12 schools before launching Project Abhay, fewer than 30% of students could correctly identify what Fundamental Rights they held. Only 12% knew they had the right to remain silent during police questioning. And just 8% knew they could challenge an illegal detention without needing a lawyer present.

What Students Actually Need to Know

Project Abhay doesn't aim to turn students into lawyers. It aims to give them a working knowledge of the rights most relevant to their everyday lives. The curriculum covers six core areas:

🗣️

Right to Expression

What you can and can't say — and what protects you when institutions push back.

🛡️

Right Against Arbitrary Detention

What police can and cannot do. Your right to remain silent. How to ask for legal aid.

📚

Right to Education

What the RTE guarantees — and what schools are legally required to provide every child.

🏛️

Right to Constitutional Remedies

That you can approach a court when your rights are violated — even if you're a minor.

🌐

Digital Rights

Privacy online, data consent, and what government and companies can legally access.

⚡

Right to Equality

Protection against discrimination — in schools, workplaces, and public spaces.

How We Teach It

The worst way to teach legal literacy is to lecture about it. The best way is to put students in situations where they have to use it.

Project Abhay uses a structured role-play format we call "The Case." Each session presents a real-world scenario — drawn from actual reported cases in India — where a young person's rights are under question. Students are divided into teams: one arguing for the individual's rights, one arguing for the institution's position, and one serving as a constitutional panel that must rule on the question.

🎭

Students running a mock constitutional hearing in Bangalore

The cases aren't hypothetical edge cases. They're situations students recognize from their own lives: a school confiscating a phone, a principal banning a political discussion, a security guard refusing entry to a public space. The constitutional questions embedded in these situations are real — and so is the learning.

Three Cases That Changed the Room

Case Study 01

The Student Newsletter

A school principal orders the student council not to publish an article criticizing the mid-day meal program. Students must argue: does a school have the authority to censor student publications? Under what Article does the student's right apply, and what are its limitations in an educational institution?

Case Study 02

The Deleted Video

A student films a road accident and is stopped by a police officer who orders him to delete the footage. He complies out of fear. Was the officer's instruction lawful? What right did the student give up — and what should he have said?

Case Study 03

The Admission Denial

A family is told their child cannot be admitted to a private school because of their religion. The family doesn't know where to start. Students must identify the violation, the relevant provision, and the legal remedy available to the family — and explain it in plain language.

What Changes After Project Abhay

We follow up with students six months after the program. The most consistent finding isn't that students remember constitutional articles. It's that they remember that they have options — that when something feels wrong, there are real mechanisms to respond.

📊

Post-program survey results: awareness and confidence scores

One student from our Hyderabad cohort told us about a moment six weeks after the program: her school tried to charge her family for textbooks that the RTE guarantees for free. She knew it was wrong. She looked up the provision, brought it to the principal's office, and the fee was waived that same day.

"I wasn't angry," she told us. "I just knew I was right. That's different from before."

Fearlessness isn't the absence of fear. It's knowing that you have somewhere to stand. That's what legal literacy gives you.

Why This Belongs in Every School

We're not proposing that schools turn into law schools. We're proposing something far simpler: that students leave school knowing the rights they were born with, and having practiced using them at least once.

A student who knows their rights isn't a troublemaker. They're a citizen. And a country of citizens who know their rights is a stronger democracy than one of subjects who don't.

Project Abhay is currently active in 34 schools across six cities. We're looking to expand. If you're an educator, school administrator, or parent who wants to bring Project Abhay to your institution, we want to hear from you.
